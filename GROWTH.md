Human brain growth is not a simple assembly line. It is a set of overlapping biological waves: cells are specified, born, migrate, differentiate, connect, compete, prune, myelinate and keep adapting. But the sequence below is a useful “biological build order”.

1. Embryonic origin: neural plate → neural tube

The nervous system begins from the ectoderm, one of the three embryonic germ layers. Around the third post-conception week, part of the ectoderm thickens into the neural plate, folds into the neural groove, and then closes into the neural tube. The neural tube becomes the central nervous system: brain and spinal cord.

A simplified sequence:

Ectoderm
→ neural plate
→ neural groove / neural folds
→ neural tube
→ brain vesicles + spinal cord

The front end of the neural tube expands into early brain vesicles:

Forebrain     → telencephalon + diencephalon
Midbrain      → mesencephalon
Hindbrain     → metencephalon + myelencephalon
Spinal cord   → caudal neural tube

These later become:

Telencephalon  → cerebral cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia, olfactory areas
Diencephalon   → thalamus, hypothalamus, retina, epithalamus
Mesencephalon  → midbrain
Metencephalon  → pons, cerebellum
Myelencephalon → medulla
2. Patterning: the brain gets coordinates before it gets circuits

Before neurons are fully produced, the developing brain is chemically “mapped”. Gradients of signalling molecules define axes such as:

front ↔ back
top/dorsal ↔ bottom/ventral
medial ↔ lateral

These gradients help decide whether a progenitor zone will make, for example, cortical excitatory neurons, striatal neurons, thalamic neurons, hypothalamic neurons, motor neurons, interneurons or glia.

Important patterning signals include Sonic hedgehog, BMPs, Wnts, FGFs and retinoic acid. The key point is that neurons do not appear randomly: their identity is strongly influenced by where and when their progenitor cells divide.

3. Progenitor zones: ventricular factories

Early brain tissue is arranged around fluid-filled ventricles. The main proliferative zones are:

Ventricular zone, VZ
→ earliest stem/progenitor layer next to the ventricle

Subventricular zone, SVZ
→ secondary proliferative region

Outer subventricular zone, OSVZ
→ especially important in large primate/human cortical expansion

The main early progenitors are neuroepithelial cells, which later transition into radial glial cells. Radial glia are crucial because they are both:

1. stem/progenitor cells
2. migration scaffolds for newborn neurons

In the human cortex, outer radial glia in the OSVZ are especially important for expanding cortical surface area and supporting the large, folded human neocortex.

4. Neurogenesis: neurons are born in timed waves

Brain development proceeds through overlapping phases: neurulation, neurogenesis, migration, axon/dendrite growth, synaptogenesis, pruning and myelination.

A simplified order is:

Neural stem/progenitor cell
→ intermediate progenitor
→ immature neuron
→ migrating neuron
→ differentiating neuron
→ mature circuit participant

Different brain regions produce different neuron types at different times. Broadly:

Early-born neurons
→ deep, long-range, foundational circuits

Later-born neurons
→ upper-layer, associative, integrative circuits

Even later waves
→ many glial cells, especially astrocytes and oligodendrocyte lineage cells

In humans, most neurons of the cerebral cortex are generated before birth, while differentiation, synaptic development, pruning and myelination continue for many years afterwards. Prefrontal cortical maturation can extend into the third decade of life.

5. Migration: neurons move to their correct destinations

Newborn neurons rarely stay where they are born. They migrate.

There are two major migration modes:

Radial migration
→ outward movement from ventricular zones to cortex
→ especially important for excitatory cortical pyramidal neurons

Tangential migration
→ sideways migration across brain territories
→ especially important for many inhibitory interneurons

In the neocortex, excitatory neurons are mostly born near the ventricle and migrate outward along radial glial fibres. Inhibitory interneurons are largely born in ventral forebrain regions, especially the ganglionic eminences, and then migrate tangentially into the cortex.

6. Cortical layering: the neocortex builds inside-out

The human neocortex has six main layers. Its excitatory neurons are generated in an inside-out sequence: early-born neurons settle in deep layers, while later-born neurons migrate past them to form upper layers.

Layer I       Molecular layer
Layer II/III  Upper-layer cortico-cortical neurons
Layer IV      Main thalamic input layer, especially sensory cortex
Layer V       Major output to brainstem, spinal cord, striatum
Layer VI      Output back to thalamus
White matter  Axons entering/leaving cortex

Developmental sequence:

Preplate
→ splits into marginal zone + subplate

Early-born cortical neurons
→ layers VI and V

Middle-born neurons
→ layer IV

Later-born neurons
→ layers III and II

Layer I
→ sparse neuron layer, including Cajal-Retzius cells early in development

So the cortex is not built like a wall from bottom to top. It is built more like:

deep layers first
upper layers later
late neurons migrate through earlier neurons
7. Major neuron classes

At a high level, the brain contains several major neuronal categories.

Excitatory projection neurons

These usually use glutamate and often send long-range axons.

Examples:

Cortical pyramidal neurons
Thalamocortical relay neurons
Hippocampal pyramidal neurons
Cerebellar granule cells

In cortex, pyramidal neurons are the dominant excitatory cell type. They are the main carriers of long-range cortical output and cortico-cortical communication.

Inhibitory interneurons

These usually use GABA and shape timing, gain, rhythm, competition and stability.

Major cortical interneuron families include:

Parvalbumin-positive interneurons
→ fast-spiking basket cells, chandelier cells
→ timing, synchrony, gamma oscillations

Somatostatin-positive interneurons
→ often target dendrites
→ regulate input integration

VIP-positive interneurons
→ often inhibit other interneurons
→ disinhibition / context gating

Neurogliaform and other interneuron types
→ slow inhibition, volume-like local modulation
Modulatory neurons

These are fewer in number but have wide influence.

Dopaminergic neurons
→ substantia nigra, ventral tegmental area
→ reward, movement, salience, learning

Serotonergic neurons
→ raphe nuclei
→ mood, arousal, behavioural state

Noradrenergic neurons
→ locus coeruleus
→ alertness, uncertainty, attention

Cholinergic neurons
→ basal forebrain, brainstem
→ attention, learning, cortical activation

Histaminergic neurons
→ hypothalamus
→ wakefulness and arousal

These systems are not just “extra chemicals”; they alter how circuits learn, stabilise, attend, explore and switch state.

8. Glial development: support cells become active partners

Glia are not passive filler. They are active participants in growth, signalling, immunity, metabolism and circuit refinement.

Main types:

Radial glia
→ stem cells and migration scaffolds

Astrocytes
→ metabolic support, neurotransmitter recycling, synapse regulation,
blood-brain barrier support, extracellular ion balance

Oligodendrocytes
→ myelinate CNS axons

OPCs, oligodendrocyte precursor cells
→ generate oligodendrocytes; remain dynamic into adulthood

Microglia
→ immune surveillance, synaptic pruning, inflammatory response

Ependymal cells
→ line ventricles; involved with cerebrospinal fluid environment

Oligodendrocyte myelination is a multistep process: precursor proliferation, migration, differentiation, then wrapping axons in multilayered myelin sheaths.

9. Axon growth and pathfinding

Once neurons are born and migrating, they extend axons and dendrites.

The axon tip has a growth cone, which behaves like a biological explorer. It responds to attractive and repulsive signals:

Attractants
→ pull axons toward targets

Repellents
→ push axons away from wrong zones

Adhesion molecules
→ help axons fasciculate or choose pathways

Intermediate guideposts
→ stepping-stone targets before final destination

Examples of guidance families include:

Netrins
Slits / Robos
Semaphorins / Plexins
Ephrins / Eph receptors
Cell adhesion molecules

Large-scale tracts form through this process:

Corpus callosum
→ left-right cortical connection

Internal capsule
→ cortex to thalamus, brainstem, spinal cord

Corticospinal tract
→ motor cortex to spinal cord

Thalamocortical projections
→ thalamus to cortex

Cerebellar peduncles
→ cerebellum to brainstem/cortex-related loops
10. Dendritic arborisation and spine formation

A neuron’s dendritic tree grows after migration and early differentiation. Dendrites create the receptive field of the cell.

For cortical pyramidal neurons:

Soma
→ basal dendrites
→ apical dendrite
→ apical tuft
→ dendritic spines

Dendritic spines are tiny protrusions where many excitatory synapses form. They are dynamic: they appear, stabilise, enlarge, shrink or disappear depending on activity and molecular signalling.

This is central to learning because many long-term circuit changes are expressed through:

spine creation
spine elimination
spine enlargement
receptor insertion/removal
synaptic strength changes
11. Synaptogenesis: circuits connect in excess

Synapses form before birth and continue forming rapidly after birth. The young brain generally creates too many connections, then refines them.

Major synapse types:

Chemical synapses
→ neurotransmitter-based
→ excitatory, inhibitory or modulatory

Electrical synapses
→ gap junction based
→ direct ionic coupling, fast synchronisation

Neuromuscular junctions
→ specialised synapses onto muscle, outside the brain proper

Main chemical synapse classes:

Glutamatergic synapses
→ usually excitatory
→ AMPA, NMDA, kainate receptors

GABAergic synapses
→ usually inhibitory in mature circuits
→ GABA-A, GABA-B receptors

Glycinergic synapses
→ inhibitory, especially spinal cord/brainstem

Dopaminergic synapses/varicosities
→ modulatory

Serotonergic
Noradrenergic
Cholinergic
Histaminergic
Peptidergic
Endocannabinoid-modulated

Important developmental detail: GABA can be depolarising early in development in many contexts because immature neurons handle chloride differently. Later, as chloride transporters mature, GABA becomes more classically inhibitory.

12. Synapse refinement: pruning, competition and stabilisation

After exuberant synaptogenesis, the brain refines itself.

Processes include:

Activity-dependent strengthening
→ useful/coherent pathways stabilise

Synaptic pruning
→ weak, redundant or mistimed synapses are removed

Programmed cell death
→ excess neurons may die if they fail to integrate properly

Microglial pruning
→ immune-like cells help remove selected synapses

Critical periods
→ windows of heightened plasticity for sensory, motor, language and social systems

The rough principle is:

overproduce → test through activity → stabilise useful patterns → remove inefficient patterns

This is one of the most important biological design principles of brain development.

13. Myelination: speed, timing and network efficiency

Myelination begins before birth but continues for decades. It does not happen everywhere at once.

General sequence:

spinal cord / brainstem pathways
→ early sensory and motor systems
→ cerebellar and subcortical circuits
→ primary cortical areas
→ association cortex
→ prefrontal networks

Myelin does more than increase speed. It also changes:

signal timing
synchronisation
energy efficiency
axon survival
network bandwidth
long-range coordination

This matters because cognition depends not only on whether areas are connected, but whether signals arrive with useful timing.

14. Regional maturation sequence

Very roughly, the brain matures from survival/sensorimotor systems toward higher associative control systems.

Brainstem
→ basic autonomic regulation, sleep-wake, breathing, heart rate

Spinal and sensorimotor pathways
→ movement, reflexes, sensory processing

Thalamus and hypothalamus
→ relay, arousal, homeostasis, endocrine/autonomic control

Cerebellum
→ motor coordination, timing, prediction; later cognitive roles

Primary sensory cortex
→ vision, hearing, touch

Primary motor cortex
→ voluntary movement control

Limbic circuits
→ emotion, memory, salience, motivation

Association cortex
→ multimodal integration

Prefrontal cortex
→ planning, inhibition, abstraction, working memory, social judgement

This is why basic perception and movement mature earlier than executive control, long-term planning and impulse regulation.

15. Simplified whole-brain biological growth sequence

A compact end-to-end sequence:

1. Ectoderm forms neural plate
2. Neural plate folds into neural tube
3. Neural tube regionalises into forebrain, midbrain, hindbrain, spinal cord
4. Ventricular progenitor zones expand
5. Neuroepithelial cells become radial glia
6. Neural progenitors produce neurons in timed waves
7. Neurons migrate radially and tangentially
8. Cortical layers form inside-out
9. Axons grow using molecular guidance cues
10. Dendrites branch and spines form
11. Early synapses form, including transient developmental connections
12. Excitatory, inhibitory and modulatory circuits differentiate
13. Astrocytes, oligodendrocytes and microglia mature
14. Synapses are overproduced
15. Activity strengthens useful circuits
16. Weak/redundant circuits are pruned
17. Axons are progressively myelinated
18. Networks continue plastic adaptation through childhood, adolescence and adulthood
16. A useful “architecture” view

For a biologically inspired system, the human sequence suggests this architecture:

Growth before intelligence:
structure emerges before advanced function

Local rules before global cognition:
molecular gradients, local competition and activity shape large systems

Overproduction before optimisation:
build excess capacity, then prune

Timing is part of information:
myelination, oscillation and conduction delay are computationally meaningful

Multiple cell classes are essential:
excitatory cells compute and project
inhibitory cells stabilise and gate
modulatory cells change learning state
glia regulate metabolism, plasticity and maintenance

Development remains open-ended:
the system is never simply “finished”; it becomes progressively constrained,
specialised and efficient

The deepest biological pattern is probably:

differentiate → migrate → connect → compete → stabilise → prune → myelinate → adapt
